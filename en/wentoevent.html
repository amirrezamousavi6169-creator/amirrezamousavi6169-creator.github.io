Case study

Wentoevent

Event discovery platform · Dubai first

How a broad lifestyle concept became one narrow product with a specific first city — and why almost nothing gets built until the supply side exists.

01

The problem

Social platforms are optimised to keep you scrolling, not to get you out of the house. The result is more screen time and fewer real plans.

Meanwhile, finding something to actually do this weekend is still solved by word of mouth and scattered Instagram pages. There is demand with no front door.

02

Why this product, and only this one

Lifestyle — the parent concept — was too broad to build. A multi-product ecosystem is a strategy, not a first release.

So the question became: which single product carries the whole thesis and can ship alone? Event discovery. It is the narrowest expression of "spend time with people, in a real place", and it stands on its own commercially.

03

Why Dubai before Iran

Dense, constant event supply, and a largely transient population — people who arrived recently and have no local network are exactly the users who need discovery most.

English-first, high spending power, and no dominant local player already owning discovery.

Iran was ruled out as the first market for structural reasons, not market ones: payment rails and app-store access make international scale impossible from day one. Launching where you can neither get paid nor distribute is a decision you pay for later.

04

The model

Two-sided: discovery stays free for attendees, because a marketplace that charges the demand side before it has supply kills itself.

Revenue comes from organiser-side membership and a per-ticket fee — not advertising. Ad revenue pays us for attention; ticket revenue pays us for attendance. Only one of those aligns with the reason the product exists.

Public publishing is gated, with a pathway organisers earn by delivering. Quality of supply is the product; an open firehose of events is a directory, not a discovery tool.

05

Validate before you build

The plan deliberately spends on validation before development: landing page, waitlist, social presence, community, and direct recruitment of founding hosts.

Target for launch day: enough live events that the first visitor finds something worth attending. An empty marketplace does not get a second visit.

It also changes the investor conversation — evidence of demand plus committed supply is a much cheaper thing to raise against than a finished app with no users.

Status

Pre-launch. Product direction, pricing structure, membership model and the four-month pre-launch plan are defined. Current focus is organiser recruitment and waitlist building ahead of the Dubai launch.
